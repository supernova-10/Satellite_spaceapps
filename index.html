Impact

Seasat operated in Earth orbit for 105 days, measuring sea-surface winds and temperatures, wave heights, atmospheric liquid water content, sea ice features and ocean topography, before a massive short circuit in the spacecraft's electrical system ended the mission on October 10, 1978.

Impact

Oceanic Ecosystem Monitoring: The AVHRR instrument also enabled the monitoring of oceanic ecosystems by providing data on chlorophyll concentrations and the presence of phytoplankton. These measurements are essential for studying primary productivity, assessing the health of marine ecosystems, and monitoring changes in oceanic biodiversity. Storm Tracking and Ocean Dynamics: TIROS-N satellites enhanced the ability to track storms and severe weather events over the ocean. This information is valuable for understanding the behavior of storms, their impact on coastal regions, and their interaction with the ocean, including storm surges and wave patterns. Instruments

Radiometer – Three L-band microwave polarimetric radiometers to measure the brightness temperature of microwave emissions from the ocean surface with a centre frequency of 1.413 GHz.

Scatterometer – L-band, polarimetric scatterometer provides information to correct for ocean surface roughness and shares the antenna with the radiometers. It has a 373 km wide swath, transmits in a protected 1260 MHz frequency, has a 4 MHz band width, and makes 5.6 measurements per second in vertical and horizontal polarizations.

Impact

Oceanic Mixing and Stratification: Aquarius data allowed scientists to study the vertical stratification of the ocean, which refers to the layering of water with different salinity and density. Understanding the processes that control stratification helps us comprehend heat and nutrient transfers, oceanic mixing, and the growth of marine ecosystems. Water Cycle Studies: Aquarius contributed to studying the global water cycle by providing information on the movement of freshwater across the Earth's surface. This data helped researchers improve models and predictions related to water availability, precipitation patterns, and freshwater resources Climate Studies: Salinity is a critical component of the Earth's climate system. Aquarius data contributed to climate studies by helping researchers better understand the role of salinity in ocean dynamics, including thermohaline circulation, which drives global climate patterns
